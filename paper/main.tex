\begin{abstract}
We propose a simple calibration recipe for cumulative-link collaborative
filtering: initialise the monotone thresholds from the empirical rating
quantiles of the training set, parameterise their spacing through a
softplus-inverse, and exclude them from $L_2$ regularisation. The recipe
matches the data marginal at training start and gives a native rating
distribution without sacrificing RMSE. Paired with per-dimension gated fusion
of side information, the gated$+$ordinal configuration attains
RMSE $0.9124\pm0.0036$ and NLL $1.2584$ across the five canonical
MovieLens-100K splits, beating both a no-fusion MF analogue
(none$+$sigmoid $0.9160$; none$+$ordinal $0.9170$) and additive fusion
(best variant additive$+$ordinal $0.9174$) on the across-split RMSE mean. A MovieLens-1M
scale-up preserves the same ordering (RMSE $0.8481\pm0.0006$,
NLL $1.184$). The contribution is practical rather than algorithmic:
standard components are combined into a reproducible, calibrated recommender
pipeline.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Collaborative filtering (CF) for explicit feedback predicts unobserved user--item
ratings from a sparse matrix. Latent-factor models based on matrix
factorisation~\cite{koren2009mf} remain standard baselines and building blocks
on MovieLens~\cite{harper2015movielens}.
Two persistent issues remain when these models are applied as a course-style
data-analytics tool rather than a production recommender. First, side
information (user demographics, item genres) is typically injected by adding a
projected feature vector to the latent embedding, with no learned mechanism to
decide when to trust the embedding versus the side information. Second,
the rating is treated as a continuous quantity even though it is in fact a
five-class ordinal variable; the resulting predictions can be RMSE-competitive
yet badly calibrated as distributions over star ratings.

\paragraph{Related work.}
The two issues above have each been addressed in isolation.
Gated fusion of latent representations with content features was
introduced by GATE~\cite{ma2019gate}, which uses a sigmoid gate to blend an
item embedding with item content; gating is also implicit in attention-based
factorisation. Cumulative-link ordinal regression for recommendation has
a long history starting with McCullagh's classical model~\cite{mccullagh1980ordinal}
and was applied to MovieLens by OrdRec~\cite{koren2011ordrec}. Two recent
papers operate in the same setting as ours: OrdNMF~\cite{gouvert2020ordnmf}
combines an ordinal head with non-negative MF, and OPRFM~\cite{zaman2025oprfm}
couples an ordinal probit head with factorisation machines on MovieLens-100K,
1M, and 10M. The cumulative-link parameterisation itself has seen renewed
interest in deep ordinal classification. Less of this literature focuses on
manifold-based visualisation of learned recommender embeddings; the standard
tools remain PCA~\cite{jolliffe2002pca}, metric MDS, and
IsoMap~\cite{tenenbaum2000isomap}.

\paragraph{Contributions.} Each ingredient (cumulative-link heads, gated
content fusion, softplus monotonicity) is itself well known; the combination
(quantile-initialised thresholds excluded from $L_2$ regularisation, paired
with zero-initialised per-dimension symmetric gating) is, to our knowledge,
new in this setting.
\begin{enumerate}
\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item Headline. A calibration recipe for cumulative-link CF: initialise
thresholds via $\theta_k=\mathrm{logit}(\hat F_k)$, recover $\delta_j$ by
inverting softplus, and exclude $\{\theta_1,\delta_j\}$ from $L_2$. The
predicted marginal matches the data marginal at $s=0$ to numerical precision.
\item Supporting design. A symmetric per-dimension gated fusion blending ID
embeddings with side-feature projections on both sides, zero-initialised so
all dimensions start at the neutral $0.5$ mixture; the larger RMSE change in
our ablation comes from this component.
\item Supporting diagnostic. A manifold-projection lens (PCA, metric MDS, IsoMap) on
post-fusion embeddings with silhouettes stratified by occupation and first-listed
genre; IsoMap recovers item-side genre structure that linear projections miss
($+0.23$ vs $+0.05$).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Definition}
\label{sec:problem}

Let $\mathcal{U}=\{1,\dots,n\}$ be the set of users and $\mathcal{I}=\{1,\dots,m\}$
the set of items. Observed ratings form a sparse set
$\mathcal{D}=\{(u_t,i_t,r_t)\}_{t=1}^{N}$ with $r_t\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}$. Each user
$u$ carries a side-information vector $f_u\in\mathbb{R}^{d_u}$ (z-scored age,
one-hot gender, one-hot occupation in MovieLens-100K, $d_u=24$) and each
item $i$ carries $g_i\in\mathbb{R}^{d_i}$ (multi-hot genre indicator,
$d_i=19$). The task
is to predict $\hat r_{ui}$ for held-out $(u,i)$ pairs, evaluated by RMSE, MAE,
classification accuracy of the rounded prediction, and where applicable the
held-out negative log-likelihood (NLL) of the predicted rating distribution.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Matrix factorisation backbone}
We start from a biased dot-product MF model. Each user and item carries a
$d$-dimensional embedding ($p_u, q_i\in\mathbb{R}^d$) and a scalar bias
($b_u, b_i\in\mathbb{R}$). The pre-head score is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:score}
s_{ui} \;=\; p'_u \cdot q'_i \;+\; b_u \;+\; b_i,
\end{equation}
where $p'_u,q'_i$ are the (possibly fused) representations defined below. With
fusion disabled, $p'_u\equiv p_u$ and $q'_i\equiv q_i$.

\subsection{Gated auxiliary fusion}
\label{sec:method:fusion}
Let $a_u=\mathrm{ReLU}(W_u f_u + b_{f,u})$ be a learned projection of the user
side features into the embedding space, and similarly $a_i=\mathrm{ReLU}(W_i g_i + b_{f,i})$
for items. We define the fused user representation as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gate}
g_u \;=\; \sigma\!\left(W_g \,[\,p_u \,\Vert\, a_u\,] + b_g\right),\qquad
p'_u \;=\; (\mathbf{1}-g_u)\odot p_u \;+\; g_u\odot a_u,
\end{equation}
where $\Vert$ denotes concatenation and $\odot$ is the Hadamard product, so the
gate $g_u\in[0,1]^d$ acts per dimension. The item-side fusion is
identical with $(q_i,a_i)$ in place of $(p_u,a_u)$. We zero-initialise both
$W_g$ and $b_g$, which makes $g_u\equiv \tfrac12\mathbf{1}$ at training start.
This gives every dimension the same neutral starting mixture between the ID
embedding and the ReLU-projected side features; the gate then opens and closes
per dimension as gradient flows. As an ablation we also consider the
additive variant $p'_u = p_u + a_u$ and the none variant
$p'_u\equiv p_u$ (and similarly for items).

\subsection{Ordinal regression head}
\label{sec:method:head}
For the head we treat $r$ as an ordinal variable with $K=5$ classes. We use the
cumulative-link model of McCullagh~\cite{mccullagh1980ordinal} with monotone
thresholds $\theta_1<\theta_2<\theta_3<\theta_4$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cum}
P(r\le k\mid s) = \sigma(\theta_k - s),\qquad
P(r=k\mid s) = \sigma(\theta_k - s)-\sigma(\theta_{k-1} - s),
\end{equation}
with $\theta_0=-\infty$ and $\theta_5=+\infty$. We enforce monotonicity by the
softplus reparameterisation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:softplus}
\theta_k=\theta_1+\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\mathrm{softplus}(\delta_j),
\end{equation}
which guarantees $\theta_k<\theta_{k+1}$ for any unconstrained parameters.
Replacing the logistic CDF in Eq.~\ref{eq:cum} with the normal CDF (probit, as
in OPRFM~\cite{zaman2025oprfm}) shifts headline RMSE by $-0.0009$ and NLL by
$+0.005$ across the five splits, both within between-split std, so we treat
the link choice as a parameterisation detail rather than a methodological
commitment.

\paragraph{Empirical-quantile initialisation (the recipe).}
We initialise from the empirical
rating distribution. Let $\hat p_k$ be the training-set frequency of rating
$k$ and $\hat F_k=\sum_{j\le k}\hat p_j$ its cumulative. Choosing
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quantile-init}
\theta_k = \mathrm{logit}(\hat F_k),\qquad
\delta_j = \log\!\bigl(\exp(\theta_{j+1}-\theta_j) - 1\bigr)
\end{equation}
guarantees $P(r{\le}k\mid s{=}0)=\hat F_k$, so the predicted marginal at the
start of training equals the data marginal exactly. The second equation is
just the softplus inverse, recovering the unconstrained $\delta_j$ that the
optimiser then updates. We also exclude $\{\theta_1,\delta_j\}$ from weight
decay because their scale carries calibration information.

The training loss is the negative log-likelihood
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ord}}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_t \log P(r=r_t\mid s_{u_ti_t})$,
and the point prediction is the expected rating
$\hat r_{ui}=\sum_{k=1}^{5}k\,P(r=k\mid s_{ui})$. As an ablation we also
consider the standard sigmoid head:
$\hat r_{ui}=4\sigma(s_{ui})+1$ trained with mean-squared error.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{Forward pass and ordinal NLL for one observed rating $(u,i,r)$.}
\label{alg:forward}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE $a_u\leftarrow\mathrm{ReLU}(W_u f_u + b_{f,u})$;\quad
       $a_i\leftarrow\mathrm{ReLU}(W_i g_i + b_{f,i})$ \hfill\COMMENT{side projections}
\STATE $g_u\leftarrow\sigma(W_g^{(u)}[p_u\Vert a_u]+b_g^{(u)})$;\quad
       $g_i\leftarrow\sigma(W_g^{(i)}[q_i\Vert a_i]+b_g^{(i)})$ \hfill\COMMENT{per-dim gates}
\STATE $p'_u\leftarrow(\mathbf{1}-g_u)\odot p_u + g_u\odot a_u$;\quad
       $q'_i\leftarrow(\mathbf{1}-g_i)\odot q_i + g_i\odot a_i$
\STATE $s\leftarrow p'_u\cdot q'_i + b_u + b_i$;\quad
       $\theta_k\leftarrow\theta_1+\sum_{j<k}\mathrm{softplus}(\delta_j)$
\STATE $P(r{=}k\mid s)\leftarrow\sigma(\theta_k-s)-\sigma(\theta_{k-1}-s)$,\;
       with $\theta_0{=}-\infty,\,\theta_K{=}+\infty$
\STATE \textbf{return} $\mathcal{L}=-\log P(r{=}r\mid s)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Complexity}
\label{sec:complexity}
The MF backbone has $(n+m)d$ embedding parameters plus $n+m$ biases; for
MovieLens-100K with $n=943$, $m=1682$, $d=128$, that is $\approx 3.4\times10^5$
parameters. Each fusion module adds a projection ($d_{u/i}\cdot d + d$) and a gate
($2d^2+d$); summed over both sides this is $\approx 7\times10^4$ parameters
(about one-fifth of the embedding budget). The ordinal head adds only
$K-1=4$ parameters. The per-rating forward cost is dominated by the gate's
$\mathrm{Linear}(2d, d)$ map, which is $O(d^2)$; the MF dot product and the
softplus thresholds remain $O(d)$. With $d{=}128$ on M1 Pro CPU one ablation
epoch is $\sim$2\,s.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Setup}
We use MovieLens-100K~\cite{harper2015movielens} on its five canonical
$80/20$ splits \texttt{u1}--\texttt{u5}, each yielding $80{,}000$ training and
$20{,}000$ test ratings on the same $943$ users and $1{,}682$ items.
Headline numbers are reported as mean $\pm$ std across the five per-split means
(each split-mean itself averages three training seeds). The visualisation and
cold-start analyses are restricted to the \texttt{u1} split. User side features are z-scored age,
two-class one-hot gender, and 21-class one-hot occupation ($d_u=24$); item
features are 19-genre multi-hot ($d_i=19$). All experiments use Adam with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and weight decay
$\lambda=10^{-5}$ on all parameters except the ordinal thresholds. We carve a
$10\%$ random slice of \texttt{u\textit{n}.base} ($\approx\!8{,}000$ ratings)
as a held-out validation set, leaving $\approx\!72{,}000$ for training; the
validation-split seed is fixed and shared across training-seed replicates so
val-split variance is not conflated with model-initialisation variance.
Early stopping uses validation RMSE only and the test set is read exactly
once after restoring the best-validation weights. Two protocols are used: headline (Tab.~\ref{tab:baselines}, \texttt{u1}
only, single seed, patience $30$) and ablation (Tab.~\ref{tab:ablation},
all five splits, three seeds, patience $10$ for sweep tractability). They
agree on \texttt{u1} gated$+$ordinal RMSE to within $\pm0.003$; their splits
and seed-counts are not interchangeable across tables. All
tables and figures regenerate from the public source repository at
\url{https://github.com/kevinzzzhu/comp8535-movielens-cf} with training seeds
$\{42,43,44\}$.

\subsection{Baselines and metrics}
We compare against three transparent baselines: (i) truncated SVD with rank
$20$ on the mean-centred rating matrix, (ii) biased MF trained with MSE, and
(iii) an NMF-style constrained MF: identical to the MF baseline with user/item
factors clipped to $\ge0$ after each Adam step (biases unconstrained). This is
a projected-gradient surrogate for the non-negativity constraint, not the
original multiplicative-update algorithm~\cite{lee2000nmf}. Metrics are RMSE, MAE, classification accuracy of the rounded
prediction, and (where the model exposes a class-probability head) the test
NLL.

\subsection{Ablation study}
\label{sec:ablation}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} supports three claims.

Gated fusion gives the larger RMSE change. Holding the head fixed, replacing
none fusion with gated drops RMSE by $0.0025$ (sigmoid) or $0.0046$ (ordinal);
replacing additive with gated drops it by $0.0054$ (sigmoid) or $0.0050$
(ordinal). A paired $t$-test across the five splits gives $p=0.001$ for
gated$+$ordinal vs additive$+$sigmoid (5/5 splits in favour) and $p=0.04$ for
the head-only contrast (gated$+$ordinal vs gated$+$sigmoid); the headline
gated$+$ordinal vs none$+$sigmoid gap ($\Delta=-0.0036$) is marginal
($p=0.08$, 4/5 splits in favour), reflecting that this contrast bundles head
and fusion changes. Additive fusion is consistently the worst sigmoid-head
cell, suggesting that unconditional summation of side information can hurt
once bias terms absorb marginal effects.

The ordinal head buys native probabilities, while its RMSE effect depends on
fusion. Switching from sigmoid to ordinal moves RMSE by at most $0.0015$ in any
fusion regime, all within between-split std. What it reliably buys is native
rating probabilities and per-class accuracy: it improves MAE by $0.003$--$0.007$
and accuracy by $0.004$--$0.010$ in every fusion regime.

Ordinal NLL is low across all ordinal cells. The proposed configuration
achieves NLL $1.2584$, well below the $\log 5\approx1.609$ uniform baseline.
The quantile initialisation is the mechanism: the predicted marginal matches
the data marginal at $s=0$ to numerical precision.

Figure~\ref{fig:reliability} renders the calibration curve. To evaluate the
gated$+$sigmoid head under the same definition we turn its point estimate
$\hat y$ into a five-class distribution
$P(r{=}k\mid\hat y)\propto\exp(-(k-\hat y)^2/2\sigma^2)$ with $\sigma{=}1$.
Pooled ECE is close (kernelised sigmoid $0.0108$ vs ordinal $0.0143$), but the
per-class breakdown is more informative: the ordinal head's ECE is lower on
classes $1$ ($0.009$ vs $0.025$), $2$ ($0.012$ vs $0.036$), $3$
($0.019$ vs $0.034$), and $5$ ($0.026$ vs $0.036$), with the kernelised
sigmoid winning only on the dominant class $4$. The
pattern mirrors the per-class F1 result below: sigmoid$+$MSE concentrates mass
on the dominant rating, which makes both its accuracy and its calibration look
good there and worse everywhere else; the ordinal head spreads probability
across the five classes more evenly.

\subsection{Scale-up to MovieLens-1M}
\label{sec:ml1m}
On MovieLens-1M ($1{,}000{,}209$ ratings, random $80/10/10$ split, three seeds,
same optimiser and hyperparameters, with dataset-specific side-feature
encoding) the ordering is preserved: no-fusion sigmoid
$0.8567\pm0.0008$, gated$+$sigmoid $0.8502\pm0.0003$, and
gated$+$ordinal $\mathbf{0.8481\pm0.0006}$ (MAE $0.6630$, Acc.\ $0.4654$,
NLL $1.184$). The larger data set also tightens seed variance.

\subsection{Discussion}
The ablation paints a sharper picture than the headline number alone. Gated
fusion accounts for most of the empirical RMSE change; the ordinal head provides
native probability estimates cheaply but does not help RMSE on its own,
suggesting ordinal heads are most useful when paired with side-information
mechanisms. The calibration trade-off is explicit: the ordinal head loses on
pooled ECE ($0.0143$ vs $0.0108$ kernelised sigmoid) but wins on four of five
per-class ECE values, redistributing calibration error from the extreme
classes toward the dominant class~$4$, a desirable direction for a rating
recommender.

Limitations. The additive row in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} is the closest
matched-architecture control; a no-fusion baseline with the same parameter
count as gated fusion is missing, so the gated-over-none gain conflates fusion
with capacity. The benchmark is small ($100$K ratings), the side information
is limited to demographics and genres, and the ordinal head is not modelling
per-user heteroscedasticity.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We presented a calibration recipe for cumulative-link collaborative filtering:
initialise the monotone thresholds from the training-set rating quantiles,
parameterise their spacing through a softplus-inverse, and exclude them from
$L_2$ regularisation. Paired with per-dimension gated side information, the
resulting model attains RMSE $0.9124\pm0.0036$ and NLL $1.2584$ across the five
MovieLens-100K splits, improving the across-split mean over both a no-fusion MF
analogue and additive fusion ($p=0.001$ paired over the five splits for the
gated$+$ordinal vs additive$+$sigmoid contrast). The main lesson is that gating
drives the RMSE gain while the ordinal head redistributes calibration error
toward the dominant class at negligible parameter cost. The most informative
next experiment would be a matched-parameter-count no-fusion control to
separate gating from the extra capacity it brings, together with a hybrid CF
baseline (e.g.\ OPRFM, LightFM) trained under the same protocol.
